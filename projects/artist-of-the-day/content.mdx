## The idea

Artist of the Day is a small daily practice for developing taste. The app shows
one artist, three representative works, and a few prompts that help a young designer
notice what is actually good: composition, restraint, rhythm, contrast, material,
proportion, originality, and control.

The goal is not to make art history feel like school. It is to train the eye through
repeated exposure and guided attention.

## Why it exists

Young designers often collect references faster than they can understand them. They
know what looks cool, but they may not yet have language for why something works,
why something feels unresolved, or what a better designer would learn from a great
piece of art.

This app gives them a quiet daily rep. Open it, meet one artist, look closely, name
one strong choice, and leave with a sharper visual vocabulary than they had before.

## Core loop

1. Open the app.
2. Meet today's artist.
3. Read a short note on why their work matters.
4. Study three works with one focused looking prompt each.
5. Save a taste note or share the artist.

The loop should feel complete in three minutes. No endless feed, no lesson map, no
leaderboard, and no guilt-driven streak mechanics.

## Train your eye

The most important feature is a recurring prompt layer that teaches users how to see.
Instead of explaining everything, each artist should ask a few precise questions:

- What is the strongest choice in this work?
- Where is the restraint?
- What would make this worse?
- What feels dated, and what still feels alive?
- What would a weaker designer copy from this?
- What should a better designer learn from it?

That last question is the heart of the product. If today's artist is Rothko, the weak
takeaway is "use blurry rectangles." The better takeaway is that large simple forms
can carry emotional weight when proportion, color, and edge are handled with extreme
sensitivity.

## What the app teaches

Each artist becomes a lens for taste:

- Mondrian: grid, reduction, balance.
- Hokusai: motion, framing, and line economy.
- Hilma af Klint: system, symbol, and abstraction.
- Rothko: scale, atmosphere, and restraint.
- Barbara Kruger: type, power, and contrast.
- Agnes Martin: repetition, quietness, and precision.

The app should help users move from vague preference to useful language. "I like this"
becomes "the composition is carrying the image," "the detail is earned," or "this is
polished but not expressive."

## Product shape

The first version should stay deliberately narrow:

- A daily artist page.
- A concise "why they matter" note.
- Three works with title, date, medium, source, and a single looking prompt.
- A personal taste note: "what I would learn from this."
- Saved artists.
- A simple archive of previous days.

The app should not start with comments, quizzes, social following, AI chat, or a large
recommendation feed. Those can come later if the daily ritual proves useful.

## Content principles

Good curation matters more than coverage. The first month should mix familiar anchors
with less obvious references across geography, medium, gender, and era. The point is
not to present a perfect canon. The point is to help a designer build a private canon
and learn why they trust it.

The writing should be grounded in museum pages, open collections, and cited sources.
AI can help draft summaries and looking prompts, but the app should preserve source
links and avoid unsupported claims.

## MVP plan

Start with seven hand-curated artist days. Make the experience feel complete before
automating the pipeline.

Success looks like a user remembering yesterday's artist, saving one note, and saying
the app made them feel a little more visually awake.
